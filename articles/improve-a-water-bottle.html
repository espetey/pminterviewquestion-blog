Find a water bottle. You have one? Great. Tell me how you would improve it.

Wednesday, July 1, 2020

Today's question comes from a tweet threat by Kaz Nejatian (https://twitter.com/CanadaKaz/status/1279062633489010688). The gist of the thread is that this question is useful in detecting whether the interviewee is interested in the variety of possible customers the product could be improved for. This is now one of the frameworks in my little tool belt that I need to make sure I always keep in mind. As I continue to practice for PM interview questions, one thing I think is good to internalize is to intuitively reach for a framework or structuring method before answering a question. No matter how obvious or innocuous, having some thought process shows that, for higher stakes questions, you're able to pause and reflect. This also plays into something I read along the lines of, Product Managers are most valuable when they help others come to a decision. So, in a way it is the PMs job to help find all possible options and make sure all users are represented. After that, the engineering, sales, marketing, finance, etc. departments will get to have their say as well.

Since I've got a cheap disposable bottle of water from the convenience store here, first I'd like to ask, who should we be improving the water bottle for? If we are improving it for people who drink from the water bottle, I would make it more easily collapsible and reusable. During my time trail running in Colorado, I learned that a lot of people use inexpensive plastic water bottles rather than fancier options because they can be crushed down to a small size, stored, and refilled later in the run. Doing this would increase the total value realized by the user. If we are improving it for the buyer of the bottle, I would make the bottle rectangular to provide the greatest amount of water possible given the storage limitations. This would give buyers the impression that they were getting their money's worth. I would use this same improvement for those managing the logistics and storage of the water bottles. Similar to the design of shipping containers, square water bottles would allow carriers and delivery drivers, for example, to deliver more total revenue of water in the equivalent transportation load.

For people who sell water bottles, I would add more graphic design to the bottle's exterior. Many substitutes for bottled water now exist: sparkling water, soda, juice, energy drinks, alcohol. For the purchasing demographic that simply walks up to the cooler and chooses the lowest price water, the exterior will make little difference. But, for the person who enters the store undecided about what drink they will choose, an exciting design or one which appeals to a highly active persona, similar to energy drinks, may be effective in convincing customers to choose water over one of the non-water or non-sparkling substitutes. Lastly, if this were being improved for the manufacturer of water bottles, I would switch from using plastic entirely to using something biodegradable and sourceable from recycled materials, like a lightweight recycled cardboard that could be lined on the inside with a waterproof seal. This would allow the manufacturer to participate in the increasingly important green economy and to divest from less environmentally friendly plastic products. This would hopefully also decrease manufacturing costs as source materials could be less expensive given their recycled nature and could be less hazardous given the health implications of working in a factory that melts down plastics.

If we were to pick from any of these potential users, I would suggest favoring the adoption of a square or rectangular water bottle. In addition to the direct benefits for purchasers perceiving more value and for the distributors transporting more per shipment, this form factor would be an interesting marketing opportunity to differentiate from other products on shelves.
