How do you think we came up with the price of $99/year for Hey email?

Friday, July 17, 2020

Today's question gets into a little bit of estimation. As I build out the data sheet at pminterviewquestion.com, I'll probably include a few blocks of common industry pricing structures. So, email, cloud storage, etc., would be included there so you can get a good idea of generally what those look like. Hopefully, unless you're applying in such an industry, they'd be relatively forgiving if you didn't have competitor pricing memorized.

My response

I think Hey analyzed a few things to come up with their $99/year pricing strategy. First, I believe they looked at what it would cost to host and serve a single customer in a cloud environment. This would set the floor for what they would need to price to make the business viable. An email account is essentially a file storage account where most files are text-based with some attachments. For many people, it is customary to store emails indefinitely. Hey works around this by "providing" auto-delete after a certain period of time which helps keep the data requirements for any one customer low. A company like FastMail provides a data cap, similar to Dropbox. FastMail's lowest tier is $36/year for 2GB of space. Interestingly, Dropbox is $120/year for 2TB of space and it provides, at a very high level, very similar services as an email provider. While this should not an apples to apples comparison, from a purely data-based comparison Dropbox offers rates of $0.06/GB/year, Hey offers $0.99/GB/year, and FastMail offers a whopping $18/GB/year. While Dropbox is at the low-end, I think it's safe to say they still provide some of the most robust, technically sound applications and infrastructure in the industry. So, pricing likely has little to do with pure technical costs.

Second, I think Hey looked at all the other business costs involved with each customer such as support, engineering, customer acquisition costs, and customer development costs (developing relationships with existing customers who are at risk of abandoning their account). It appears that Hey is targeting organic word-of-mouth growth for the initial stage of Hey's life. Jason Fried, the CEO, has made the rounds on nearly every tech podcast and also earned a lot of free press when the app conflicted with Apple's App Store policy. Support and engineering costs, however, can get large quickly. If Hey had 1,000 customers, their revenue would be $99,000. And, it would certainly cost more than $99,000 to employ a few each of support and engineering staff to support those 1,000 customers. At $99, they are likely aiming for at least 10,000 customers to make revenue reach $1 million and make their business achieve critical scale.

Third, I think Hey looked at its competitors and priced itself at the high-end of consumer email. As stated earlier, FastMail offers consumer-level email between $36 and $60 per year and offers a number of features such as hosting email accounts from your own domain. GSuite provides the entire family of Google products including Gmail (also hosted against a domain). Only when you get into business email do prices exceed $100. So, I believe Hey is positioning itself as the highest quality consumer-grade email provider, second only to business-class email which is naturally expected to have similar privacy features Hey advertises.

Finally, I think Hey considered what it would need to make to earn a healthy profit to continue the business without needing to raise money. Jason Fried, CEO of Hey, is very outspoken about the desire at Hey and at Basecamp to avoid venture capital or dreams of a huge IPO. From that perspective, the goal may have been to price Hey high enough to get a certain threshold of users that are price insensitive enough to pay for the service. But that threshold must eliminate more demanding, price sensitive customers who would consume greater support and customer development resources.
